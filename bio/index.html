Description of lung cancer

Lung cancer is the leading cause of cancer deaths around the world (Mayoclinic, 2020). Lung cancer is a cancer that affects the lungs but is known to be able to spread to other parts of the body including the liver, brain, bones, and adrenal glands (MedlinePlus, 2020). Lung cancer can be divided into two main types, small cell lung cancer and non-small cell lung cancer (American Cancer Society , 2019). These names are based on the size of the affected cells under a microscope (MedlinePlus, 2020). Approximately 80-85% of all lung cancer cases are non-small cell lung cancer and 10-15% are small cells (MedlinePlus, 2020, American Cancer Society , 2019). There are also a few other types of tumors including lung carcinoid tumors (less than 5% of cases) but they are far less common than the main types (American Cancer Society, 2019). Lung cancer usually starts in the cells that line the bronchi, bronchioles and alveoli (American Cancer Society , 2019). Lung cancer occurs because of mutations in the cells (MedlinePlus, 2020). When you have cancer the cancerous cells have a mutation that has caused the cell to uncontrollably multiply to form a tumor (MedlinePlus, 2020).

How lung cancer affects the body

Signs and Symptoms may include:

  • Cough that is persistent or is worsinging (Mayo clinic, 2020, American Cancer Society, 2019)
  • Coughing up blood and mucas(Mayo clinic, 2020, MedlinePlus, 2020)
  • Coughing up rust-colored sputem (American Cancer Society, 2019)
  • Shortness of breath (Mayo clinic, 2020)
  • Chest pain (Mayo clinic, 2020). Chest pain may also be worse when breathing deeply, coughing, and laughing (American Cancer Society, 2019).
  • Unintended weight loss (Mayo clinic, 2020)
  • Pain throughout the body including the lining of the lungs and bones (Mayo clinic, 2020) Pain in the bones (possibly in hips or back) is usualy caused once the cancer spreads to other parts of the body (American Cancer Society, 2019).
  • Respitory infections such as pnemonua and bronchitis that are persistently come back or dont go away (American Cancer Society, 2019)
  • Starting to wheeze (American Cancer Society, 2019)
  • Loss of apitite (American Cancer Society, 2019)
  • Trouble swallowing (MedlinePlus, 2020)
  • Trouble speaking (MedlinePlus, 2020)

Diagnosis

There are many ways to diagnos lung cancer. Screening is recommended by the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF) to only do screening if Have a history of heavy smoking (smoking 30 packs a day for a year), and Smoke now or have quit within the past 15 years, and Are between 55 and 80 years old. (Centers for Disease Control, 2020) AND Task force also recommends STOPPING SCREENING if Turns 81 years old, or Has not smoked in 15 or more years, or Develops a health problem that makes him or her unwilling or unable to have surgery if lung cancer is found.

  • Have a history of heavy smoking (smoking 30 packs a day for a year) AND
  • Smoke now or have quit within the past 15 years, AND
  • Are between 55 and 80 years old. (Centers for Disease Control, 2020)

The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force also recomends stoping screening if

  • the patient is 81 years old or older, or
  • Has not smoked in 15 or more years, or
  • Develops a health problem that makes him or her unwilling or unable to have surgery if lung cancer is found (Centers for Disease Control, 2020)
